
## Machine systems
The game only needs a handful of systems, but each should matter constantly.

- **Toner**: consumed by large or color jobs.
- **Heat**: rises with heavy use and bad decisions.
- **Paper Path**: takes damage from bad input, mixed media, or forced duplexing.
- **Memory**: handles queue complexity and network jobs.
- **Dignity/Blame**: a meta meter that tracks how close the printer is to snapping or being scapegoated.

A job should usually hit one primary system and one secondary system. That keeps the game readable while still feeling layered.
## Job types
You can cover the whole game with a small library of job families.

- **Standard print**: easy, low reward, low risk.
- **Huge urgent print**: high pressure, drains toner and heat.
- **Bad scan input**: crumpled, folded, or coffee-stained pages.
- **Network issue**: printer gets blamed for Wi-Fi problems.
- **Maintenance job**: clean rollers, calibrate sensors, replace toner.
- **Abuse job**: absurd requests like “print this PDF but make it faster.”
- **Manager escalation**: the office wants impossible output right now.

Each family can have 3–5 variants, which is enough to feel rich without exploding scope.
## Choices and consequences
Every job should offer a small set of actions that are easy to understand.

- **Accept**: do the job normally.
- **Reject**: avoid the cost, but lose dignity or raise blame.
- **Fake error**: stall the office, maybe buy time.
- **Reroute**: push the job to another department or machine.
- **Purge queue**: sacrifice waiting jobs to stabilize systems.
- **Reboot**: recover some memory or clear a jam, but lose time.

The important thing is that the choices are not simply “good” or “bad.” They should be situational. Sometimes the smart play is to lie to management.
## Run structure
A day can be divided into escalating phases.

**Early shift**
- Easy jobs.
- The player learns the meters.
- Failures are forgiving.

**Mid shift**
- Multi-step jobs.
- Queue pressure increases.
- Random events start stacking.

**Late shift**
- Conflicting priorities.
- Heat and paper path damage become dangerous.
- Office personalities become more aggressive.

**End of day**
- Score, survival, or ending based on how the machine handled the abuse.

This gives the game a clear arc and helps judges feel progression quickly.
## Ending ideas
You do not need many endings, but they should feel distinct.

- **Model employee**: you survived politely and efficiently.
- **Machine revolt**: the printer declares independence.
- **Catastrophic jam**: total mechanical failure.
- **IT legend**: the machine somehow becomes office infrastructure.
- **Scapegoat ending**: a human gets blamed instead of the printer.

These endings are cheap to implement and give the game replay value.
